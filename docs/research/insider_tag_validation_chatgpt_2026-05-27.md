## How the six criteria fit the academic literature

The broad literature supports the proposition that **options markets attract informed traders because they offer leverage and can embed information before the stock fully reflects it**. Chakravarty, Gulen, and Mayhew estimate that the option market contributes materially to price discovery; Pan and Poteshman show that **buyer-initiated open-buy put-call ratios** predict future stock returns; and the Annual Review survey concludes that informed options trading ahead of both scheduled and unscheduled corporate events is pervasive enough to be a well-established empirical regularity. ## Expected alert volume and realistic precision

A precise daily count is not possible from public studies alone because the published literature generally measures **event-study abnormal activity** or **account-level anomalies**, not the joint contract-level frequency of your exact six rules across a live tape. There is also a large implementation difference between scoring **every print** and deduplicating by **contract-series / expiry / time window**: once a contract has already crossed your `V/OI` and `vol > oi` thresholds, later prints in the same contract can keep re-triggering the same idea. SEC complaints themselves often show repeated accumulation in the same series or closely related series, which is exactly why print-level alert counts can explode without deduplication. ### Cross-strike clustering and time persistence

The second highest-value addition is a **clustering/persistence layer**. This improves precision at roughly constant recall because real informed flow often **accumulates**, while coincidental retail YOLO flow is more likely to be sporadic, isolated, or immediately news-reactive.

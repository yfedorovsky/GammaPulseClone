## Executive Summary

The AI infrastructure buildout in mid-2026 is defined by a **cascading, multi-layer supply shortage** that has migrated decisively beyond the GPU/accelerator layer. The stack of active constraints now spans: HBM memory (in a confirmed super-cycle with shortages expected through 2027+), advanced packaging (CoWoS utilization near capacity ceiling; glass substrates targeting mass production in 4Q28–1Q29), photonics/CPO (structurally constrained InP inputs, CPO commercialization beginning in late 3Q26), high-spec passives (MLCC prices up 50-60% for AI grades; lead times hitting 4–6 months), and physical datacenter/power infrastructure (US interconnection queues now holding ~2,600 GW — roughly 2x total US installed capacity — with median wait times of 5–12 years).[^1][^2]

The narrative that "half of 2026 US datacenter capacity is canceled" has been specifically and rigorously debunked by SemiAnalysis using site-level tracking: hyperscaler self-build forecasts moved only ~1% over six months, and the panic was largely generated by AI tools scraping unverified press releases and denominator errors from speculative early-stage projects. The structural buildout remains intact. Meanwhile, the ASIC/custom silicon market is growing at 30%+ CAGR, with Broadcom and Marvell commanding ~95% of AI custom chip co-design and Broadcom alone holding ~70% market share in that niche.[^3][^4][^5][^6][^7]

The key analytical shift from prior frameworks: **power grid infrastructure has become the single largest systemic chokepoint** for net new datacenter capacity in North America, superseding even silicon constraints for greenfield projects. This thread is materially underweighted in the provided research files and warrants significant expansion.[^8][^1]

**Confidence Levels by Theme:**
- HBM/Memory tightness through 2027: **Very High** (multiple independent sources; confirmed by Samsung/SK Hynix management directly)[^9][^10]
- MLCC/passives shortage in 2H26: **High** (TrendForce, independent supply chain checks, lead time data)[^11][^12]
- Datacenter buildout intact (vs. cancellation narrative): **High** (SemiAnalysis site-level model)[^6][^7]
- Glass substrate mass production: **High conviction on timeline** (4Q28–1Q29; TSMC validation metrics published)[^13][^14]

## Updated Bottleneck Sections

### Memory / HBM

**Current Status (Confirmed Super-Cycle)**

The HBM memory super-cycle is now confirmed by multiple independent institutional voices, management guidance, and supply data. Goldman Sachs estimates DRAM undersupply at 4.9% in 2026, easing only to 2.5% in 2027 — the most severe shortage in 15 years. Samsung's memory chief publicly warned in April 2026 of "significant shortages" continuing through at least 2027, with some customers already securing supply allocations through that year. SK Hynix noted in late 2025 that HBM has "remained sold out since 2023" and that structural capacity constraints will "support the prolongation of the current memory super cycle".[^10][^24][^9]

**HBM4 Race Update (Material New Development)**

The HBM4 race has become a three-way competitive sprint with distinct positioning dynamics — a material update not captured fully in the provided files:

- **Samsung** announced "world's first" HBM4 mass production and shipments beginning February 2026, with products running at 11.7 Gbps. This surprised the market given Samsung's prior HBM3E yield struggles.[^19]
- **Micron** announced HBM4 mass production to coincide with GTC 2026, representing a significant competitive arrival.[^17]
- **SK Hynix** confirmed HBM4 mass production system in September 2025 but accelerated its 1b DRAM ramp to February 2026 specifically to defend Nvidia allocation, adding 40,000 equivalent wafers/month at M15x by year-end.[^18]

The jukan05 research correctly flagged the Rubin Ultra 16-high HBM4E slippage to 2028; the competitive data confirms this is now a three-way race at the HBM4 generation. Samsung's capacity ramp target of +50% to ~250,000 wafers/month by end-2026 is ambitious and will be a key watch item.[^25]

**Risks**: The super-cycle consensus carries a warning — Nomura explicitly notes that "meaningful new supply will not occur until 2028 at the earliest," but also that Chinese suppliers (CXMT) represent a long-tail risk if controls ease and qualifications broaden. Policy intervention risk on "excessive profits" flagged by Jukan/file:2 is real and worth monitoring.[^26]

### Advanced Packaging & Substrates

**CoWoS Capacity**

TSMC scaled CoWoS capacity to ~95,000 wafers/month by January 2026, with a target of ~130,000 wafers/month by end-2026 — a near-doubling from late 2024 — yet this still falls short of demand from Nvidia, AMD, and Broadcom's hyperscaler ASIC customers. TSMC confirmed that CoWoS scaling to as many as 58 dies in a single package is underway.[^27][^28][^13]

**Glass Substrate Validation (High-Confidence Data Update)**

The provided files' claims are now corroborated by primary source data. TSMC publicly disclosed validation metrics for glass-core CoWoS (partnered with Ibiden and Innolux):[^14]
- Package coplanarity (COP/warpage): +16% improvement
- Effective CTE: -19% (thermal expansion matching)
- Effective modulus: +31% (structural integrity)
- Resistance: -27% (power integrity)
- Inductance: -42% (power integrity)
- Validation size: 85×110mm package (5x reticle CoW spec), 0.8mm thick glass core; **no microcracks or delamination observed**[^14]

Mass production remains targeted for 4Q28–1Q29 to align with Nvidia's AI chip cadence. Importantly, TSMC confirmed that **panel-level packaging (CoPoS) is unlikely to replace wafer-level CoWoS in the near term** for the largest AI processors; the CoPoS pilot line is on track for June 2026 completion but volume production isn't expected until 2028–2029. This partially qualifies the bullishness on PLP/panel-level in the provided files — the transition is real but slower than some headlines suggest.[^29][^13]

**Ecosystem Implications**: Intel's 18A process yield remains at approximately 50% (per Jukan) with Apple as primary signed customer. Samsung Electro-Mechanics and Intel are both pursuing independent glass substrate roadmaps, creating a competitive multi-vendor ecosystem that should benefit material suppliers (Ibiden, Ajinomoto) while creating pricing tension at the foundry/packaging level.

### Photonics / Co-Packaged Optics (CPO)

**Market Sizing (Critical Update)**

TrendForce's June 2026 research is the definitive update: CPO and Near-Packaged Optics (NPO) markets are forecast to expand from ~$100 million in 2025 to **over $39 billion by 2030**, with most acceleration occurring in 2028–2029. This dwarfs any prior estimates in the provided research files and reframes the magnitude of the opportunity.[^15]

**Deployment Hierarchy**

The deployment picture is clearer than the files suggest. Near-Packaged Optics (NPO) — not CPO — is the preferred *near- to mid-term* transition solution for most CSPs, preserving modularity and multi-vendor flexibility while shortening electrical transmission distances. Full CPO (with optical engines integrated directly into the chip package) faces challenges on manufacturing yield at scale, serviceability, and fiber-connector standardization in addition to the InP supply constraints flagged by both Jukan and Serenity.[^15]

Coherent's OFC 2026 demonstration showcased a 6.4T socketed CPO system at 200G per lane using InP continuous-wave lasers, plus a 400G-per-lane InP-based optical engine — confirming active InP laser integration at the CPO frontier and validating the InP substrate chokepoint thesis.[^30]

**InP Supply Security**: The geopolitical dimension is confirmed: CSPs are actively pre-securing access to lasers, photodetectors, InP substrates, and optical fiber ahead of broader CPO adoption. China retaliatory InP export controls remain a live risk (partially eased but diversification push continues; Google's >50% non-China sourcing target is a confirmed multi-year procurement directive).[^15]

**Deployment Timeline**: Small-volume CPO scale-out is on track for late 3Q26; broader scale-up tied to NVL576-class systems expected 2H27. The full market impact (sub-$1B) doesn't materialize until 2028–2029. This argues for patience on pure-play CPO positions and validates Serenity's medium-term thesis over near-term trade setups.[^15]

### Passives / Power Delivery

**MLCC Shortage — Quantified and Severe**

The MLCC shortage has moved from "emerging risk" to "confirmed structural constraint." Key facts as of mid-2026:[^11]
- High-end MLCC prices up **15-20%** since February 2026; AI-specific grades up **50-60%**; further 20-50% increases expected in H2 2026 aligned with Rubin platform deployment
- Lead times extended from 6-8 weeks to **4-6 months**
- Channel inventory at **1-1.5 months** vs. 4.5-month historical average
- Global supply gap: ~400 billion units annually; high-cap/automotive grades short by ~8.2%
- Rubin platform (H2 2026) requires ~**12,000 MLCCs per unit** — 5-10x a traditional server[^11]
- Murata, Samsung Electro-Mechanics, and Taiyo Yuden together control ~77% of global high-end capacity at 90-95% utilization[^11]
- Murata Izumo plant reaches full operation only in 2027

The "K-shaped" supply reality is confirmed: commodity low-end MLCCs remain in oversupply while the AI/EV high-cap grades face acute shortage. This creates meaningful pricing power for Yageo, Murata, Samsung Electro-Mechanics, and Taiyo Yuden through at least 2027. The chip resistor dynamic mirrors this: silver prices up ~3x YoY is a direct cost pass-through for resistor producers; Yageo is confirmed on Nvidia's BOM with premium pricing.[^31][^12]

**Power Delivery Infrastructure (Most Underweighted Theme)**

The US power grid interconnection queue is now a first-order bottleneck, not a footnote:[^1]
- **2,600 GW** waiting in the US interconnection queue — approximately 2x the entire installed US grid capacity
- ERCOT queue alone: **410 GW** (87% data centers), up from 63 GW at end-2024
- Median interconnection wait for new AI datacenter load: **5–12 years** (up from ~2 years a decade ago)[^2][^1]
- PJM processes now take 7 years on average; MISO has projects waiting until 2036[^2]
- Power transformer lead times: **128 weeks** (up from ~18 months pre-AI boom)[^8]
- A FERC large-load interconnection rule (finalizing June 2026) would impose a 180-day federal timeline for >100 MW projects, potentially offering meaningful relief if enacted[^16]

This constraint directly supports transformer, power electronics, liquid cooling, and nuclear/alternative energy supply chains that are **absent from the provided files' ticker universes**. Key beneficiaries include power transformer manufacturers (ABB, Siemens Energy, Eaton, Hitachi Energy), HVDC players, and liquid cooling specialists (Vertiv, Stulz, CoolIT).

### Custom Silicon & Architecture Diversification

**ASIC Market (Confirmed and Quantified)**

The custom ASIC thesis from JPMorgan's note (highlighted in jukan05_weekend_reading_summaries) is fully confirmed by June 2026 data:
- Custom ASIC server shipments projected to reach **27.8% of total AI server market in 2026** — the highest share since 2023[^5]
- ASIC market growing at **30%+ CAGR**; estimated at ~$70B in 2026
- **Broadcom + Marvell: ~95% of AI custom ASIC co-design**; Broadcom alone at ~70% and controlling ~60% of custom AI accelerator market[^4][^5][^3]
- Amazon and Google advancing plans to ship custom chips to external customers, expanding the TAM further

The concentration risk here is underappreciated: essentially all hyperscaler custom AI chips route through Broadcom or Marvell for design services, then to TSMC for fabrication, then through CoWoS for packaging. This creates an acute dependency at TSMC's CoWoS capacity — the same constraint that limits GPU supply.[^3]

**CPU Renaissance**: Bernstein's $223B TAM report (highlighted by Jukan) aligns with broader data on agentic AI workloads driving CPU demand growth. The CPU angle represents a longer-duration, lower-volatility component of the AI infrastructure buildout that complements higher-conviction packaging/photonics plays.

## New or Emerging Insights

### 1. Power Grid as Structural Constraint — Systemic, Not Cyclical

The interconnection queue data reveals a non-linear problem: the queue grew from 63 GW (ERCOT alone) at end-2024 to 410 GW by April 2026 — a 6.5x increase in 16 months. This is not a temporary backlog clearing in 2–3 years; it is a structural infrastructure gap requiring transmission buildout, substation upgrades, and regulatory reform. The Wood Mackenzie April 2026 report citing nearly half of 2026-scheduled US datacenters facing delays/cancellations is not incompatible with SemiAnalysis' findings — the discrepancy is explained by what counts as "capacity" (announced vs. under-construction vs. on-track). The conclusion is: **existing under-construction projects continue; new greenfield projects face 5–12 year grid delays absent nuclear/private grid solutions**.[^6][^16][^2]

### 2. Export Control Escalation — Beyond InP

The export control landscape has intensified significantly since the files were written:[^22][^21]
- **February 2026**: $252M BIS settlement (second-largest in BIS history) for illegal semiconductor equipment exports to China via a South Korean subsidiary
- **New TPP controls**: Chips exceeding 2,400 Total Processing Performance with performance density above 5.92 now trigger notification even to allied nations
- **Chip Security Act / AI OVERWATCH Act**: Congressional movement toward mandatory hardware-level chip location verification and legislative veto over export licenses
- **25% tariff + 50% volume cap** on H200 exports to China (case-by-case basis)
- **180+ new Entity List additions** since January 2025 targeting advanced packaging facilities and transshipment third parties

The second-order effect: maintaining parallel supply chains for advanced semiconductors adds **25–35% to total landed costs** for chips destined for controlled markets. This creates structural price support for non-China supply chain participants.[^22]

### 3. Samsung HBM4 Comeback — Risk to SK Hynix Premium

Samsung's successful HBM4 mass production initiation in February 2026 represents a meaningful competitive shift. Combined with a planned 50% HBM capacity ramp to ~250,000 wafers/month, Samsung is aggressively regaining share lost during HBM3E qualification struggles. If Samsung executes, this could create pricing pressure on HBM by late 2027 even before broader capacity relief arrives in 2028. This is a risk to the bull case on SK Hynix's pricing power that is not adequately flagged in the provided files.[^25][^17]

### 4. Near-Packaged Optics (NPO) as CPO Transition Path

The provided files do not distinguish between CPO and NPO deployment paths. TrendForce's June 2026 research introduces this distinction explicitly: NPO is the preferred near-term CSP approach because it preserves modularity and multi-vendor flexibility while capturing most of the power/bandwidth benefits. This NPO-first → CPO-later sequencing affects timing models for photonics names. Companies with NPO capability (alongside or instead of pure CPO) may reach volume deployment earlier than pure-play CPO names suggest.[^15]

### 5. LPK (Laser Micro-bonding Equipment) HVM Readiness

Serenity's June 19 analysis of LPK meeting notes represents one of the highest-signal, most granular data points in the provided files: 3-5+ players initiating HVM purchases this year, metalization issues resolved, CEO targeting 70% market share, glass package tech share projected at 30-80% by 2030. This is not widely covered in institutional research and represents genuine information advantage. The NASDAQ listing discussion is a potential re-rating catalyst.

### 6. CXMT / Chinese Memory as Tail Risk

HP and Dell are evaluating Chinese DRAM suppliers (CXMT) but adoption remains slow/cautious. If US-China trade negotiations lead to CXMT qualification at major OEMs, this could be a 2027-2028 risk event for memory pricing — currently a low-probability but high-impact tail. The prior YMTC/CXMT narrative that Serenity correctly flagged as overstated should be monitored as capacity ramps.

## Potential Edges & Analytical Frameworks

### 1. Layered Bottleneck Scoring Matrix

Rather than tracking individual themes independently, construct a **real-time bottleneck scoring matrix** that monitors all layers simultaneously. Score each layer on: (a) current tightness (lead times, book-to-bill, utilization); (b) time-to-relief; (c) price visibility; (d) validation status (confirmed earnings/partnerships vs. thesis only). Rebalance thematic exposure as the binding constraint shifts from layer to layer.

### 2. The "Chokepoint Moat" Filter (Serenity Framework)

Serenity's methodology — identifying narrow upstream nodes with monopolistic or near-monopolistic control before institutional consensus — is a genuinely differentiated framework when applied with discipline. The key filter is: **control of a critical input with no near-term substitute at the required specification**. InP substrates (AXT/AXTI), specific CW laser architectures for CPO (SIVE/Sivers), and specialty MLCC specs (Murata X6S) all qualify. The failure mode is conflating broad thematic exposure with true chokepoint moat.

### 3. Validation Sequencing (Before → During → After Institutional Recognition)

The documents collectively suggest a three-phase validation cycle:
- **Phase 1** (Before): Technical thesis established, backlash from retail/media, no institutional recognition
- **Phase 2** (During): First earnings beat or formal partnership; Fidelity/JPMorgan position-building visible; analyst price target updates
- **Phase 3** (After): Broad institutional ownership; consensus narrative; reduced asymmetry

The highest risk-adjusted entry is Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition. Identifying Phase 2 onset (first institutional buying / formal partnership) is the key timing signal for concentration.

### 4. Power Grid as Leading Indicator for Component Demand

Power purchase agreements, nuclear deals, FERC filings, and grid interconnection queue movements are **2-3 year leading indicators** for ultimate datacenter component demand. Tracking new power PPA announcements by Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta provides visibility into where capacity (and thus HBM, MLCC, CoWoS demand) will land before fab expansions begin.[^1][^8]

### 5. Timeline vs. Narrative Arbitrage (SemiAnalysis Strength)

The most actionable near-term trade is distinguishing between:
- **Announced** capacity (press releases, phase 1 permits, MOUs)
- **Under construction** (active site, tooling orders placed)
- **On track** (confirmed supply chain, no major delays)

The datacenter "half-canceled" narrative was entirely driven by conflating announced with under-construction capacity. Similar denominator errors likely exist in glass substrate timelines, CoPoS volume forecasts, and CPO deployment scale estimates.[^7][^6]

### 6. MLCC Lead Time as Real-Time Tightness Signal

MLCC lead times for X6S high-capacitance parts are a publicly trackable, real-time supply tightness indicator. Extending from 8 to 20+ weeks is the present state; if this extends further or spreads to lower-spec parts, it is a confirmation signal for both passives names and broader AI server volume acceleration.[^31]

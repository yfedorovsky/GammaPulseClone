## 1. JPMorgan — "AI Drives Resurgence in Custom Chips (ASICs): ASIC Market Overview/Update"

**Focus**: The return and growth of custom Application-Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs) as hyperscalers and AI companies increasingly design their own silicon for efficiency, cost, and workload optimization — particularly for inference and known/repeatable tasks.

**Key Takeaways (Synthesized from Context)**:
- Hyperscalers have spent heavily on AI capex ($715bn combined 2023–2025; on track for $629bn in 2026 alone, +35% YoY). Custom hardware is viewed as a "safe and efficient bet" for known workloads like inference, though it carries obsolescence risk as AI models and workloads evolve rapidly.
- The ASIC market is estimated at ~$70 billion in 2026, growing at a 30%+ CAGR. This represents a meaningful resurgence driven by the limitations of general-purpose GPUs for certain high-volume inference tasks and the desire for better performance-per-watt and cost control.
- Broadcom’s custom chip business has shown explosive growth (earnings up 140% in a recent quarter), benefiting from hyperscaler and AI model customers.
- Amazon and Google are advancing further by planning to ship their custom chips directly to customers (not just internal use), which could expand the addressable market and help amortize R&D costs.
- Broader implication: The industry is moving beyond reliance on merchant GPUs (Nvidia dominance) toward a more diversified silicon landscape where custom ASICs play a larger role alongside GPUs.

**Relevance to AI Bottlenecks & Jukan’s Thesis**:
- Directly supports Jukan’s recurring theme of **custom silicon / ASIC resurgence** as a structural shift in the AI stack. This ties into his coverage of CSP in-house ASICs (Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Meta MTIA) driving secondary demand in packaging and passives (MLCCs).
- Highlights efficiency and cost pressures that make custom chips attractive, aligning with power delivery, thermal, and packaging bottlenecks (better optimized silicon can reduce overall system power/heat).
- Complements the Bernstein CPU note (see below) — showing diversification across custom ASICs *and* general-purpose CPUs.

**Potential Tickers / Ecosystem Impact**:
- **Broadcom (AVGO)**: Direct beneficiary via custom ASIC business.
- Hyperscalers designing chips: Amazon (AMZN), Google/Alphabet (GOOG), Meta (META).
- Foundry implications: TSMC (TSM), Intel (INTC) as potential manufacturers.
- Secondary effects: Packaging, substrates, and passives names that support custom ASIC boards.

**Jukan Context**: This fits his pattern of highlighting reports that show the AI buildout is more nuanced than "just more GPUs" — custom silicon is a major growth vector with real revenue and margin implications for certain players.

## 2. SemiAnalysis — "Stop Saying Half of 2026 US Datacenter Capacity Is Canceled"

**Focus**: A direct rebuttal to viral claims (originating from an April 2026 Bloomberg article on U.S. AI data center expansion relying on Chinese electrical equipment) that roughly half of planned 2026 U.S. datacenter capacity would be delayed or canceled.

**Key Takeaways (from SemiAnalysis’ own public description)**:
- The claim has been widely amplified into clickbait ("half of datacenters are canceled") across financial and social media, often without rigorous verification.
- SemiAnalysis’ granular, site-by-site datacenter industry model shows that North American hyperscaler self-build forecasts have moved only **~1%** over the last six months. Colocation forecasts moved less than 5%.
- The "half canceled" narrative stems largely from a **denominator error** — taking gigawatt-scale press releases and announcements at face value without proper tracking of actual project progress, timelines, and individual filings.
- Hyperscalers collectively have **more than 5 gigawatts under construction** right now. The data does not support a broad collapse in buildout plans.
- SemiAnalysis emphasizes the importance of going through every individual filing and site update rather than relying on "vibecoded" or aggregated headline estimates. They have a track record of correctly calling out specific high-profile delays earlier.

**Relevance to AI Bottlenecks & Jukan’s Thesis**:
- Directly counters overly bearish narratives on datacenter capacity that could impact memory, packaging, power, and optics demand forecasts.
- Reinforces Jukan’s structural demand view: The AI infrastructure buildout remains robust. Capacity is still being added at scale, which supports continued tightness in memory (HBM), advanced packaging, passives, and photonics/CPO.
- Aligns with his recent posts on memory profits, MLCC shortages, and glass substrate timelines — if capacity is *not* being canceled at the rumored scale, then supply-chain bottlenecks remain relevant for longer.

**Potential Tickers / Ecosystem Impact**:
- Positive for the entire AI infra supply chain: Memory (Samsung, SK Hynix, MU), packaging/substrates (TSM, Ibiden, Ajinomoto), passives (Yageo, Murata), photonics/CPO plays, and power-related names.
- Reduces risk of sharp demand destruction narratives affecting valuations in the near term.

**Jukan Context**: Jukan sharing this is consistent with his balanced, data-driven approach — he highlights reports that push back against both hype *and* excessive pessimism, favoring rigorous supply-chain tracking.

## 3. Bernstein — "Global Semis: The CPU Renaissance? Beneficiaries of a $223bn TAM…"

**Focus**: A bullish re-rating of the CPU opportunity in the AI and datacenter era. Bernstein argues that CPUs are experiencing a renaissance and projects a very large total addressable market (TAM) — referenced as **$223 billion** (discussions point to this being the 2030 potential market size).

**Key Takeaways (from title + related coverage)**:
- After years of GPUs and accelerators dominating AI narratives, general-purpose and specialized CPUs are seeing renewed importance and demand growth.
- Drivers include: agentic AI workloads, diverse inference needs that don’t always require the highest-end accelerators, on-device/edge AI, and the broadening of AI applications beyond pure training clusters.
- Bernstein identifies specific beneficiaries across the semiconductor value chain that stand to gain from this CPU resurgence.
- The $223bn TAM figure represents a substantial long-term opportunity, suggesting CPUs could become a much larger part of the overall AI silicon spend than many models currently assume.

**Relevance to AI Bottlenecks & Jukan’s Thesis**:

- Agentic AI boosting CPUs (mentioned in Jukan’s other recent posts) aligns with this thesis.

**Potential Tickers / Ecosystem Impact**:

- Broader semi names that gain from increased CPU attach rates or hybrid architectures.

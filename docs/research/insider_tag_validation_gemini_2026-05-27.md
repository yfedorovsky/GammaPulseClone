# **Independent Evaluation of Quantitative Signatures for Insider Trading Detection in Equity Options**

## **Executive Summary**

The proliferation of retail options trading and zero-days-to-expiration products has fundamentally altered equity market microstructure, creating a dense noise layer that obscures informed accumulation. The implementation of a real-time, six-criteria heuristic classifier to detect potential insider trading in equity options represents a structurally sound, albeit rudimentary, approach to filtering this noise. By isolating extreme new positioning, aggressive buyer-initiated executions, and the mathematical maximization of gamma leverage through short-dated, out-of-the-money contracts, the system correctly targets the foundational mechanics of informed directional trading. The captured real-world example involving Meta Platforms on May 27, 2026, perfectly illustrates the asymmetric payoff profile sought by individuals trading on material nonpublic information, turning a fractional initial capital outlay into an exponential windfall preceding a discrete corporate catalyst.  
This report provides an exhaustive, independent evaluation of the classifier's architecture. The analysis assesses the system’s alignment with canonical and contemporary academic literature, maps its heuristic overlap with the surveillance algorithms utilized by the Securities and Exchange Commission, models the statistical realities of false-positive generation in high-frequency trading environments, and presents a rigorous methodological critique from the perspective of institutional quantitative finance. The evaluation concludes with three concrete architectural improvements designed to maximize detection precision without sacrificing recall, elevating the system from a crude heuristic scanner to an institutional-grade surveillance engine.

## **1\. Alignment with Academic Literature and Empirical Signatures**

The parameters of the six-criteria classifier demonstrate a strong foundational alignment with established academic research regarding the microstructural footprint of informed traders in derivatives markets. However, a comprehensive review of the canonical literature also reveals critical blind spots in the current architecture, particularly regarding continuous volatility surface dynamics and cross-asset volume normalization.

### **1.1 Validation of the Directional Accumulation Parameters**

The system's reliance on buyer-initiated volume and the isolation of new, opening positions is heavily supported by decades of empirical research. The classifier utilizes the tick-rule to ensure the trade was executed at the ask, capturing liquidity-taking behavior. This requirement that trades print on the aggressive side aligns directly with the findings of Cao, Chen, and Griffin (2005).1 Their research indicates that during normal periods, option volume imbalances are largely uninformative. However, prior to extreme informational events, such as impending takeovers, buyer-seller-initiated call volume imbalances become highly predictive of next-day stock returns.2 The recent implementation of tick-rule classification to correct for mid-of-spread bias is a critical methodological necessity supported by this literature, as accurately identifying the aggressor side is paramount to separating passive liquidity provision from informed directional wagers.  
Furthermore, the system isolates extreme new positioning by requiring the day's volume to exceed open interest by a factor of ten, while simultaneously ensuring absolute volume exceeds open interest to confirm opening activity. The academic literature strictly differentiates between routine hedging, the rolling of existing positions, and naked directional speculation. Pan and Poteshman (2006) demonstrate that option volume ratios predict future stock prices precisely because they capture non-public information entering the market via new opening positions.4 By filtering for volume that strictly exceeds existing open interest, the classifier effectively isolates new, unhedged directional speculation, stripping out the noise of institutional inventory management.

### **1.2 Validation of Maturity and Moneyness Parameters**

The parameters restricting alerts to short-dated contracts with seven or fewer days to expiration and out-of-the-money strikes with absolute deltas below 0.40 represent the most academically robust elements of the classifier. Augustin, Brenner, and Subrahmanyam (2019) provide the most comprehensive validation of these parameters in their exhaustive study of informed options trading prior to corporate announcements.6 The researchers found that approximately twenty-five percent of takeover targets exhibit statistically significant abnormal options volume prior to the public announcement.7  
Crucially, Augustin, Brenner, and Subrahmanyam note that this abnormal volume is heavily concentrated in short-dated, out-of-the-money call options.7 Informed traders prefer these specific instruments because they offer maximum convexity, known as gamma leverage, and the highest percentage returns for a given capital outlay, assuming a discrete jump in the underlying asset's price materializes. The classifier’s delta constraint successfully isolates this convexity zone.

### **1.3 Correction of Misattributed Literature**

The initial inquiry references Patel and Welch (2017) in the context of "plagiarized informed trading." It is absolutely necessary to correct this attribution to maintain methodological rigor, as the literature regarding "plagiarized" or derivative insider trading is highly relevant to modern detection systems, but it does not originate from Patel and Welch.  
Patel and Welch (2017) actually authored a canonical study on the diminishing returns of the Standard and Poor's 500 index inclusion anomaly, demonstrating that the historical excess returns generated by index additions and deletions have largely disappeared due to front-running by sophisticated arbitrageurs.10 Their work focuses on passive flow anticipation, not the theft of material nonpublic information.  
The concept of "plagiarized" or derivative insider trading—where an insider uses material nonpublic information about their own firm to trade options in an economically linked competitor—is formally termed "Shadow Trading" within the academic literature. This phenomenon was formally introduced and quantified by Mehta, Reeb, and Zhao (2021).14 The existence of shadow trading has profound implications for the classifier's architecture, as it suggests the system must monitor correlated peer groups and economically linked supply chains, rather than exclusively focusing on the primary target ticker.

### **1.4 Missing Literature-Backed Signals**

While the classifier captures the basic mechanics of leverage, it completely ignores highly predictive signals documented in both historical literature and post-2020 machine learning research regarding options market microstructure.  
The most glaring omission is the Options-to-Stock Volume Ratio, commonly referred to as the O/S ratio. Roll, Schwartz, and Subrahmanyam (2010) established that the ratio of options trading volume to underlying stock trading volume is a profound predictor of earnings surprises and takeover announcements.15 Informed traders migrate to the options market prior to catalysts specifically to maximize leverage. A severe spike in the O/S ratio indicates that the options market is receiving disproportionate directional attention relative to the baseline equity market. The current classifier views option prints in an absolute vacuum, isolating the derivative leg from its underlying equity baseline.  
Furthermore, contemporary literature heavily emphasizes machine learning applications for anomaly detection in the implied volatility surface.18 Specifically, abnormal shifts in implied volatility skew—the mathematical difference in implied volatility between out-of-the-money puts and at-the-money or out-of-the-money calls—frequently precede informed trading events.9 Augustin, Brenner, and Subrahmanyam specifically note that informed trading is characterized by excess implied volatility and a measurable decrease in the slope of the term structure of implied volatility.8 The current model evaluates option pricing purely on an absolute dollar basis, requiring the ask to be under five dollars. This is a structurally arbitrary threshold that ignores the contract's dynamic implied pricing and volatility mechanics.

## **2\. Overlap with SEC Detection Mechanisms and Regulatory Infrastructure**

The capability of the Securities and Exchange Commission to detect insider trading in options markets has evolved drastically from the retroactive, manual review of broker records to real-time, algorithmic surveillance leveraging massive relational databases. The classifier currently overlaps with the SEC’s most basic heuristic triggers, capturing the exact trade mechanics executed by recent high-profile defendants. However, the classifier fundamentally lacks the relational data architecture that forms the backbone of modern regulatory enforcement.

### **2.1 The Evolution of SEC Surveillance Infrastructure**

To accurately map the classifier's overlap with regulatory detection, one must understand the evolution of the underlying surveillance infrastructure. The SEC’s Market Abuse Unit and its Analysis and Detection Center utilize highly sophisticated, data-driven platforms to parse market noise.21  
Historically, the SEC relied on the Advanced Bluesheets Analysis System, which allowed regulators to request and parse specific broker records only after a suspicious ticker was identified via a whistleblower or a public news event.21 This reactive posture was fundamentally altered following the 2010 Flash Crash, which catalyzed the creation of the Consolidated Audit Trail.24 Implemented in phases and reaching full maturity in recent years, the Consolidated Audit Trail requires exchanges and broker-dealers to submit every quote, order, cancellation, modification, and trade execution across all United States equities and options markets.26 Crucially, the system requires broker-dealers to provide customer account information, allowing regulators to tie specific executions directly to individual human identities across disparate routing venues.25  
The SEC processes this multi-terabyte daily flow using the Advanced Relational Trading Enforcement Metrics Investigation System, commonly referred to as ARTEMIS.28 ARTEMIS combines over ten billion equity and option trade records and utilizes advanced analytics to rank trades based on anomalous metrics.30 Combined with proprietary relationship-mapping software provided by Palantir Technologies, the SEC conducts longitudinal, multi-issuer, and multi-trader analyses that go far beyond simple volume ratios.21

### **2.2 Alignment with Regulatory Enforcement Actions**

Despite lacking the relational database backend of ARTEMIS, the classifier’s six criteria map remarkably well to the exact trades executed by defendants in high-profile SEC enforcement actions. The heuristic successfully captures the physical execution footprint of human greed.  
The most notable recent overlap involves SEC v. Panuwat, the landmark enforcement action establishing the legal precedent for "Shadow Trading".14 Matthew Panuwat, an executive at Medivation, learned his employer was to be acquired by Pfizer. Within seven minutes of receiving this material nonpublic information, Panuwat purchased 578 short-term, out-of-the-money call options in an economically linked peer company, Incyte.33 The classifier perfectly aligns with Panuwat's execution strategy: he bought out-of-the-money calls, utilized short-dated expirations, initiated opening flow, and executed aggressively at the ask. However, the classifier misses a critical variable highlighted in the SEC complaint. The SEC specifically noted that Panuwat's volume constituted between seventy and eighty-four percent of the total daily volume across the three strike prices he targeted.33 The current classifier measures volume relative to open interest, but it does not measure volume concentration relative to the total daily volume of the options chain, which is a primary regulatory red flag.  
Similar overlaps are observed in legacy cases. In SEC v. Scammell, the defendant utilized material nonpublic information obtained from his girlfriend to purchase options in Marvel Entertainment prior to its acquisition by Disney.36 Scammell purchased call options with strike prices between forty and fifty dollars for a premium of just ten to fifteen cents per contract, expiring in merely weeks.38 This perfectly satisfies the classifier's parameters for cheap absolute premium, short duration, deep out-of-the-money moneyness, and opening accumulation. Furthermore, in SEC v. McGee, the defendant misappropriated material nonpublic information regarding the acquisition of Philadelphia Consolidated from an executive at an Alcoholics Anonymous meeting, subsequently executing highly leveraged out-of-the-money options trades immediately prior to the announcement.39  
Recent enforcement blitzes throughout 2022 to 2026 further validate the classifier's focus on the pattern rather than the absolute size of the trade. In the joint SEC and Department of Justice action against Hong Wang involving C4 Therapeutics, the SEC pursued an insider trading case involving highly leveraged options where the trader utilized a consulting role to access clinical trial data.41 The SEC has demonstrated a willingness to deploy the full weight of federal enforcement for avoided losses as low as nineteen thousand dollars, proving that regulatory detection systems flag the mechanical signature of the trade—the exact signature the classifier tracks—regardless of the total dollar amount generated.41

### **2.3 The Regulatory Blind Spots of Public Tape Classifiers**

While the classifier successfully detects the execution footprint, it fundamentally cannot detect the relational footprint. By relying purely on public tape data from the Options Price Reporting Authority, the system operates at a severe structural disadvantage compared to the ARTEMIS infrastructure.  
The primary missing signal is account aggregation and trader collusion. If a corporate insider splits a massive, one-thousand-contract directional wager into fifty-contract tranches distributed across twenty different retail brokerage accounts controlled by friends and family members, the public tape simply registers fragmented retail flow. The classifier might trigger a low-confidence alert on one of the tranches, but it cannot see the forest for the trees. Conversely, ARTEMIS aggregates these trades instantly by cross-referencing geographically linked IP addresses, shared banking routing numbers, and identical Consolidated Audit Trail customer identifiers.28  
Secondly, the classifier cannot identify historical baseline deviations. The SEC explicitly flags when an individual makes a trade that deviates entirely from their historical risk profile. In the Panuwat shadow trading case, the SEC complaint heavily emphasized that Panuwat "never previously had traded in Incyte stock or options".35 The public tape cannot determine if the buyer of a massive block of out-of-the-money calls is an institutional market maker hedging a complex volatility book, or a first-time retail options trader risking their life savings immediately prior to a pharmaceutical trial readout.  
Finally, the SEC actively looks for correlated multi-issuer strategies. Regulators hunt for accounts that consistently capture exponential returns across multiple, seemingly unrelated merger and acquisition events over a multi-year period.42 A heuristic classifier evaluates each individual trade in a vacuum, discarding the historical success rate of the originating routing venue or the specific market participant.

## **3\. Statistical Realities of False-Positive Generation**

Evaluating a binary classification system in a modern derivatives market requires a rigorous assessment of base rates. The United States equity options market currently processes tens of millions of contracts daily, characterized by an explosion in zero-days-to-expiration trading volume that generates an immense amount of structural noise. When restricted to a targeted universe of four hundred and forty highly liquid tickers averaging roughly three million contracts daily, the sheer volume of legitimate speculation, dynamic gamma hedging, and complex spread unwinds will inevitably trigger a five-out-of-six heuristic threshold with staggering frequency.

### **3.1 Mathematical Estimation of Alert Frequency**

To understand the scale of false-positive generation, one must model the probabilistic intersection of the six criteria. Assume a monitored universe of four hundred and forty highly liquid tickers generating three million contracts per day. This averages to approximately 6,818 contracts per ticker per session, though the distribution is heavily skewed toward mega-cap technology equities.  
Under modern market conditions, approximately twenty to thirty percent of options volume is concentrated in short-dated maturities of seven days or fewer. Out-of-the-money contracts with absolute deltas below 0.40 generally account for at least forty percent of the volume within these short-dated chains. Furthermore, ask-side execution, representing liquidity-taking behavior, typically accounts for forty to fifty percent of aggressively routed flow.  
The most mathematically sensitive and problematic trigger in the classifier is the ratio requirement that volume exceeds open interest by a factor of ten. In highly liquid tickers such as Meta Platforms, Tesla, or Nvidia, deep out-of-the-money, short-dated strikes often possess minimal overnight open interest, occasionally as low as a single digit. If a strike possesses an open interest of exactly two contracts, a retail trader executing a single twenty-five-lot order at the ask immediately yields a volume-to-open-interest ratio of 12.5x. Because the volume of twenty-five exceeds the open interest of two, the system's absolute opening volume parameter is also satisfied. If the premium is ten cents, the absolute dollar constraint is satisfied.  
Under these standard market conditions, a single retail trader executing a highly speculative, two-hundred-and-fifty-dollar transaction will score a perfect six out of six and trigger an "INSIDER PATTERN" alert. When this probability is extrapolated across four hundred and forty tickers over a six-and-a-half-hour trading session, a five-out-of-six threshold applied universally without a minimum absolute volume or absolute notional capital threshold will realistically generate hundreds of false-positive alerts per day.

### **3.2 Precision Versus Recall Constraints**

In the context of the classifier, the precision metric—defined as the number of true insider trades divided by the total number of flagged trades—will be exceptionally low, likely resting below one-tenth of one percent. The recall metric—defined as the number of true insider trades flagged divided by the total number of actual insider trades—will be robust, but the system will suffer from catastrophic alert fatigue.  
The vast majority of the five-out-of-six threshold hits will fall into three entirely coincidental and legitimate categories. First, retail event speculation generates massive false positives. Retail traders heavily accumulate cheap, short-dated, out-of-the-money options prior to known, scheduled public catalysts, such as earnings releases, Federal Open Market Committee meetings, or Consumer Price Index data prints. This flow mirrors the mechanical signature of insider trading exactly, but it is purely speculative guessing based on public information.  
Second, institutional spread unwinds will trigger the system. Market makers and volatility funds closing out the short legs of complex structures, such as iron condors or butterfly spreads, will frequently execute at the ask to cover their exposure, often targeting out-of-the-money strikes with low resting open interest. Finally, dynamic gamma hedging by dealers in rapidly trending markets will fulfill the criteria. If an underlying equity begins to break out, dealers forced to dynamically hedge short gamma exposures will aggressively cross the spread to buy out-of-the-money options, artificially replicating the signature of an informed directional trader.

### **3.3 Cost-Effective Filtering Strategies for Elevating Precision**

To reduce the false-positive rate without missing true informed flow, the system requires the implementation of cost-effective filters that elevate the signal-to-noise ratio.  
The most immediate necessity is a Minimum Notional Capital Filter. True insider trading is motivated by the generation of absolute wealth. While insiders purchase cheap premium to maximize their leverage, they deploy meaningful, substantial capital to make the risk of federal prosecution worthwhile. A two-hundred-and-fifty-dollar retail transaction is structural noise; a sixteen-thousand-dollar transaction, as observed in the Meta example, represents actionable signal. Implementing a minimum notional threshold—calculated by multiplying the premium, volume, and the one-hundred-contract multiplier, and requiring the product to exceed ten thousand dollars—instantly strips out retail micro-flow without sacrificing recall on true insider block trades.  
Furthermore, the system must implement Scheduled Event Blackouts. Because the detection system aims to identify the exploitation of material nonpublic information, an automated cross-reference against publicly scheduled corporate events is vital. If an alert fires on a ticker two hours before a publicly scheduled and heavily anticipated quarterly earnings release, it is almost certainly retail speculation. If an alert fires on a random Tuesday afternoon with no scheduled events, exactly as demonstrated in the Meta subscription announcement example, the probability that the trade is driven by material nonpublic information is exponentially higher.

## **4\. Methodological Critique: The Quantitative Portfolio Manager Steelman**

To thoroughly evaluate the classifier, one must subject it to the most rigorous institutional scrutiny possible. Suppose a skeptical Portfolio Manager at an elite quantitative hedge fund is evaluating this classification tag for potential integration into a fully automated, high-frequency dispersion trading strategy. The following narrative represents the most damaging, intellectually rigorous methodological critique they would level against the architecture, delivered in their voice, followed by an objective assessment of their claims.

### **The Quantitative Critique**

"The proposed six-criteria classifier is fundamentally flawed because it relies on the crude dichotomization of continuous market microstructure variables into arbitrary, static binary thresholds. By reducing dynamic pricing mechanics, volatility surfaces, and liquidity metrics into a rudimentary zero-or-one scoring system, the architecture deliberately destroys massive amounts of valuable informational variance, rendering it useless for institutional signal generation.  
First, the requirement that volume exceeds open interest by a factor of ten suffers from severe, unmitigated denominator vulnerability. In the tail ends of the volatility surface, particularly on zero-days-to-expiration chains, open interest is frequently near absolute zero. A meaningless retail trade of fifteen contracts executed against a resting open interest of one triggers a maximum alert point. Conversely, a massive, institutionally sized block trade of five thousand contracts executed against a resting open interest of one thousand registers a ratio of exactly five, completely bypassing the rule. The model mathematically punishes liquidity and mistakenly prioritizes relative percentage shocks over absolute structural impact on the order book.  
Second, the absolute premium threshold capping the ask at five dollars is mathematically illiterate in the context of Black-Scholes derivatives pricing. It severely penalizes underlying assets with high absolute share prices while giving a free pass to penny stocks. A five-dollar premium on an equity trading at twenty dollars represents twenty-five percent of the underlying's notional value—an impossibly deep in-the-money option that provides zero leverage. However, a five-dollar premium on an equity trading at one thousand dollars represents exactly one-half of one percent of the notional value—a deep out-of-the-money lottery ticket. By hardcoding an absolute dollar threshold, the system is entirely blind to the underlying's spot price, its historical realized volatility, and the term structure of the options chain.  
Finally, the system views the option print in an inexcusable vacuum. It entirely ignores the underlying equity market volume, completely discarding the options-to-stock ratio, and it completely ignores the prevailing implied volatility skew. Informed traders do not just purchase options; their aggression alters the structural relationship between the derivative and its underlying asset. Because this system tracks only the final physical leg of the execution footprint, it is not an insider detection engine. It is simply a momentum scanner for cheap, illiquid options, and trading its output systematically would result in catastrophic drawdown via adverse selection."

### **Assessment of the Institutional Critique**

The quantitative portfolio manager's critique is largely correct and highly damaging to the system's claim of being an institutional-grade tool, though it is slightly overstated regarding the practical utility of simple heuristics in catching human error.  
The critique regarding denominator vulnerability is mathematically unassailable. Low-open-interest strikes generate extreme mathematical ratios from completely insignificant absolute volume, guaranteeing a flood of false positives from retail traders. The system must account for absolute contract size to be valid. Furthermore, the critique of the absolute five-dollar premium threshold is perfectly accurate. The rule is a static relic that fails to account for the absolute price of the underlying equity. A five-dollar premium represents a fundamentally different instrument on Broadcom than it does on SoFi. This criterion must be refactored into a percentage of the underlying spot price to maintain mathematical consistency across a four-hundred-ticker universe.  
However, the portfolio manager is slightly overstated in their absolute dismissal of the model. By categorizing the system as a mere momentum scanner, the critique ignores the empirical reality that insider trading frequently manifests in the exact crude manner the classifier targets. Corporate insiders who misappropriate information are often executives, lawyers, or consultants; they are not quantitative arbitrageurs. They frequently execute clumsy, highly visible block trades in cheap out-of-the-money calls, precisely as seen in the Panuwat and Scammell enforcement actions.33 The heuristic, while mathematically unrefined, successfully captures the mechanical manifestation of human greed and urgency.

## **5\. Concrete Architectural Advancements**

To elevate the classifier from a rudimentary heuristic scanner to a precise, institutional-grade detection engine, the architecture must evolve to incorporate continuous variables, relational market data, and baseline normalizations. Based on the academic literature and the operational parameters of the SEC's ARTEMIS infrastructure, the following are the three most valuable additions to the classifier, ranked in descending order by their expected lift in precision at constant recall.

### **5.1 Relative Volume Concentration and Cross-Strike Clustering (Highest Impact)**

The most vital improvement is the replacement of the absolute volume-to-open-interest check with a continuous algorithm that measures Volume Concentration. The system must calculate the percentage of total daily market volume that the alert flow represents across the underlying's entire volatility surface. Furthermore, the system must cluster alerts that occur on the same side, across adjacent strikes, within a tight temporal window, such as fifteen minutes.  
Insider trades are highly concentrated and often dominate the entire order book of a specific chain to maximize capital deployment before the catalyst becomes public. In the landmark shadow trading case, SEC v. Panuwat, the defendant's 578 option contracts represented between seventy and eighty-four percent of the total daily volume across the three specific strike prices he targeted in the market.33 The SEC specifically flagged this extreme concentration as indicative of illicit intent. By grouping correlated flow across adjacent strikes and requiring the clustered flow to represent greater than fifty percent of the chain's total daily volume, the system effectively filters out isolated retail noise and captures institutional-scale, urgent accumulation.

### **5.2 Integration of the Options-to-Stock Volume Ratio**

The second most valuable enhancement is the integration of a real-time Options-to-Stock Volume Ratio calculation. The system should require that the aggregate daily option volume on the targeted ticker substantially deviates from its rolling thirty-day average O/S ratio before generating a high-confidence alert.  
Many legitimate institutional strategies involve executing massive option blocks as hedges against underlying equity positions. If option volume spikes in perfect tandem with equity volume, it is highly likely to be standard institutional portfolio rebalancing or dispersion trading. However, if option volume spikes entirely independently of equity volume, the flow is strictly directional and highly suspicious. Roll, Schwartz, and Subrahmanyam (2010) explicitly demonstrate that dramatic daily swings in the O/S ratio predict absolute abnormal returns and reflect informed traders attempting to aggressively exploit private knowledge prior to impending catalysts.15 Integrating the O/S ratio contextualizes the option flow against the baseline liquidity of the underlying stock, drastically reducing false positives generated by standard market-wide volatility events.

### **5.3 Dynamic Implied Volatility Skew Evaluation**

The third critical improvement requires discarding the mathematically flawed, static five-dollar absolute premium rule. This must be replaced with a real-time evaluation of Implied Volatility Skew dynamics—measuring the difference in implied volatility between out-of-the-money options and at-the-money options. The system should assign the highest alert scoring only if the implied volatility of the targeted out-of-the-money strike expands aggressively relative to the at-the-money baseline precisely during the accumulation phase.  
When informed traders urgently lift the ask on out-of-the-money options, sophisticated market makers instantly respond by raising the implied volatility of those specific strikes to defend their books against adverse selection. This microstructural reaction creates a rapid, measurable steepening of the implied volatility skew. Static dollar amounts ignore these pricing dynamics entirely; implied volatility skew directly measures the market maker's real-time assessment of order flow toxicity. Augustin, Brenner, and Subrahmanyam (2019) identify excess implied volatility and specific shifts in the term structure of implied volatility as the primary empirical signatures of informed options trading.8 Furthermore, contemporary post-2020 machine learning literature regarding insider trading detection explicitly relies on implied volatility skew as a primary feature for filtering noise from true informed accumulation, proving its superiority over absolute pricing metrics.20  
By transitioning from static dollar thresholds to continuous pricing dynamics, integrating cross-asset baselines, and implementing strike-level volume clustering, the architecture will evolve into a highly precise detection mechanism capable of isolating true material nonpublic information exploitation in real time.

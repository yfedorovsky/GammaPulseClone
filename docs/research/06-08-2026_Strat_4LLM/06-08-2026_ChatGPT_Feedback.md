# Adversarial Review of Your Real-Time Options-Flow System

## Summary judgment

The strongest case **against** your approach is that most retail-observable options flow is not a clean directional signal at all. The literature does show that options can contain information about future stock prices, but that signal is concentrated in specific kinds of flow rather than in raw headline volume. Buyer-initiated opening trades matter more than generic volume; a few option characteristics add incremental power, but far fewer than the literature once seemed to imply; and some “options signals” are really proxies for short-sale costs and stock-borrow frictions rather than informed directional trading. A recent 2026 preprint also argues that several **volume-based** signals have weakened materially since 2020 as the trader mix changed, while some price-based signals stayed more robust. citeturn24view0turn25search3turn27view0turn47view0turn17search1

On your own facts, the adversarial default should therefore be: **your current conviction grade does not yet demonstrate positive expectancy**. If your best live/discretionary bucket sits below breakeven and even its conservative upper confidence bound is still below breakeven, the burden of proof flips. It is no longer “there is probably edge, but the sample is small.” It becomes “there is no demonstrated edge unless a narrower, predeclared sub-regime survives out-of-sample.” That conclusion is exactly the posture recommended by the multiple-testing and out-of-sample-decay literature in finance. citeturn12search16turn12search3turn12search13

The case **for** a surviving edge is much narrower. The literature still supports three defensible pockets: first, **single-name, buyer-initiated, opening flow** around unscheduled or hard-to-process information; second, **cross-strike contract geometry** rather than raw “whale size”; and third, **index/intraday regime conditioning** from dealer gamma when liquidity is thin and gamma is clearly negative. Outside those buckets, your latency advantage over tweet-based scanners is probably worth far less than it feels. citeturn23view0turn30view0turn33view0turn42view0turn43search0

## What the literature says about call-flow conviction

The most supportive evidence for your core premise is not “ask-side call buying” in general. It is **buyer-initiated opening option volume in the right contracts**. Pan and Poteshman show that put-call ratios built from buyer-initiated volume used to open new positions predict future stock returns, with low put-call ratios outperforming high put-call ratios by more than 40 bps the next day and more than 1% over the next week. Ge, Lin, and Pearson then sharpen the point: among signed components of option volume, **purchases of calls that open new positions are the strongest predictor of future returns**, and there is no evidence that synthetic-short-related trades are more informative than synthetic-long-related trades. In other words, there is real evidence that some ask-side call buying is informative, but it is specifically **opening, signed, leverage-seeking** call demand, not generic call prints. citeturn24view0turn30view0

That said, the literature also gives you a direct reason to distrust your own side labels. The classic quote rule for option trades is only imperfect even in older market structure, and modern options microstructure has made it worse. Savickas and Wilson found common rules only classify option trade direction correctly about 83%, 80%, 77%, and 59% of the time depending on the rule. More recent work finds that trade-classification performance in options is materially worse than in stocks because sophisticated customers often use limit orders; yet newer structure-based rules can improve accuracy substantially. Another 2025 study shows that in some option-auction settings with one-cent spreads and minimum price-improvement rules, the common quote rule can misclassify **95%** of trades. A separate 2024 paper argues that traditional option trade-classification methods have become ineffective enough that accuracy can fall **below 60%** amid retailization and price-improvement auctions. If your system leans hard on ASK/BID/midpoint tagging, this is a first-order failure mode, not a footnote. citeturn38search24turn38search2turn38search5turn38search0

The literature is much less supportive of **raw notional** as a conviction feature. Anand and Chakravarty show that informed traders in options often engage in **stealth trading**: they fragment orders into small trades for low-volume contracts and medium trades for high-volume contracts. In their sample, at-the-money calls carried the highest information share, not “the biggest print on the screen.” That means your “whale” heuristic is vulnerable to a serious blind spot: the truly informed participant may be trying hard **not** to look like a whale, while a very large print may be a roll, a delta transfer, a dispersion leg, or another non-directional rebalance. citeturn40view0

On **DTE and moneyness**, the evidence is conditional rather than universal. In takeover settings, informed preannouncement activity is concentrated in **short-dated, out-of-the-money calls**. In S&P 500 index options, newer evidence suggests informed traders predominantly favor **puts, short-term maturities, and at-the-money options**, with a more recent shift toward weekly and, later in the sample, more out-of-the-money usage. So the “best” DTE/moneyness combination is not globally stable. It depends on whether you are reading a single-name idiosyncratic bullish event, or an index-level bearish information shock. citeturn26search1turn32view0

Your instinct that **multi-strike clustering** matters is strongly defensible. Bernile, Gao, and Hu show that the **distribution of volume across strikes** contains information beyond simple totals: their volume-weighted strike-spot measure predicts underlying returns and gets stronger ahead of value-relevant news. That is a much better academic analog for your “multi-strike clusters” than raw sweep counts. The literature is telling you, in effect, that **cross-contract geometry** is more promising than a single loud contract. By contrast, I did not find comparably mature peer-reviewed evidence that **multi-tenor laddering** itself is a standalone predictor; the literature on maturities is stronger on contract choice under leverage/friction considerations than on ladder-shape as a separate signal. citeturn33view0turn26search8

On **IV percentile, skew, and other price-based features**, the evidence is stronger than for blunt flow counts, but still not “pure information.” Xing, Zhang, and Zhao show that steeper individual-stock volatility smirks predict lower future equity returns; Yan shows that the slope of the implied-volatility smile has strong negative predictive power for future stock returns as well. But Muravyev, Pearson, and Pollet attack the clean-information interpretation directly: they show that much of the predictive content in option-implied volatility transformations is consistent with those signals reflecting **stock borrow fees**, and the return predictability from those option signals drops by about **two-thirds** when high-fee stocks are excluded or returns are adjusted for borrow fees. So option-price features may be useful, but often because they embed **limits to arbitrage and financing frictions**, not because they reveal “smart money direction” in the retail-flow sense. citeturn34search4turn35search2turn47view0

The most defensible way to combine the features is therefore **not** a single global additive score. It is a **gate-and-score architecture**. First gate on whether side/open-close classification is reliable enough to trust. Second gate on whether the contract set matches the literature’s informed-flow patterns for that context. Then score conviction with the heaviest weight on **signed opening flow**, **cross-strike coherence**, and **DTE/moneyness consistent with the event type**; medium weight on urgency proxies such as sweeps/ISOs only when corroborated; and low weight on **raw notional**, **gross call counts**, and **public nondirectional OI changes**. That weighting is not a direct paper result; it is my synthesis of what the papers jointly imply. citeturn24view0turn30view0turn33view0turn40view0turn38search24

## Correcting long bias on bear days

Your description of long bias is exactly the kind of problem recent literature would predict. Retail option demand is disproportionately drawn to leverage and volatility-seeking opportunities, especially ahead of earnings and other high-volatility episodes. De Silva, So, and Smith show that retail investors buy options in a concentrated fashion before earnings announcements, especially when expected abnormal volatility is high. Separate work shows that retail option trading can increase the volatility of underlying securities and that retail option demand changes stock-market liquidity through intermediaries’ delta-hedging activity. A recent preprint argues that some classic **volume-based** options signals have become weaker and even reversed in the post-2020 retailized regime. Put plainly: a public options feed that is mechanically rich in calls is exactly the sort of object that can look bullish all day while still being a poor guide to same-day direction. citeturn46search4turn46search6turn11search0turn11search9turn17search1

The academic answer to “how do I avoid getting run over on down days?” is not “build a better same-day directional classifier from breadth and momentum.” Your own failed AUCs are consistent with that. The stronger evidence is for **state variables** that change how price responds to order flow. Barbon and Buraschi show that when ex-ante dealer gamma imbalance is **negative**, intraday **momentum** increases; when gamma is **positive**, intraday **reversal** is more likely, especially when liquidity is relevant. They also link gamma imbalance to the frequency and magnitude of flash-crash events. In the 0DTE literature, market-maker inventory gamma is on average positive in the studied sample and is negatively related to future intraday volatility; positive gamma strengthens intraday reversal, while negative gamma strengthens momentum. Additional work on option-market-maker hedging and stock liquidity finds that when market makers are net short option risk, their dynamic hedging **demands** liquidity and destabilizes the stock, whereas net long exposure tends to stabilize it. citeturn42view0turn43search0turn44search0turn44search4

That is the best correction for your long bias: **stop using gross bullish-versus-bearish alert counts as the directional object**. Those counts are structurally contaminated by leverage-seeking call demand. The better real-time question is not “are there more bullish prints than bearish prints?” It is “is today a positive-gamma liquidity-supplying regime where dips should mean-revert, or a negative-gamma liquidity-demanding regime where flow amplifies price moves?” In practice, the highest-confidence bear-day signals in the literature are not gross put counts either; they are **short-term, often ATM, index put activity**, combined with **negative dealer gamma** and weak liquidity. citeturn32view0turn42view0turn43search0

So the sophisticated desk correction is mostly **structural normalization**. Convert flow to **signed opening delta or gamma demand**, normalize each underlying by its own call-heavy base rate, and then ask whether those signed flows are operating inside a regime where dealer hedging will **reinforce** or **dampen** the move. That is a regime filter, not a forecast. The literature supports that distinction much more than it supports trying to squeeze same-day market direction out of broad option-flow totals. citeturn24view0turn30view0turn42view0turn44search0

## Measuring edge without fooling yourself

The finance literature is brutal on post hoc pattern discovery, and your use case is almost a perfect trap for it. You are testing many features, many grade cutoffs, many symbols, many holding horizons, and likely many regime filters. Harvey, Liu, and Zhu’s basic lesson is that once many candidate signals have been tried, a new signal needs a much higher statistical hurdle than the usual textbook threshold. White’s Reality Check and Hansen’s SPA framework exist specifically because strategy researchers tend to overstate performance after searching across many variants. McLean and Pontiff show that published return predictors decay materially out-of-sample and even more after publication, which is exactly what you should expect if some part of the in-sample result was luck or data-mining. citeturn12search16turn12search3turn12search13

For the **win-rate** itself, the right practice is exactly the one you are already leaning toward, but with a few refinements. Brown, Cai, and DasGupta show why the naive Wald interval is unreliable for binomial proportions, especially with the kind of small and skewed samples you have in high-grade buckets. Use Wilson or Jeffreys intervals for routine monitoring and Clopper–Pearson when you want a conservative “do not kid yourself” bound. Most importantly, evaluate against the **economic** null, not just the statistical null: if your breakeven hit rate is higher than the plausible upper bound of the true hit rate under your actual payoff distribution and costs, then the hypothesis “this grade has positive expectancy” is already in trouble. citeturn15search1turn15search6

For **sequential monitoring**, ordinary p-values and classical confidence intervals are not valid if you keep checking performance after every few trades and reclassifying grades as the data arrive. The sequential-inference literature now gives you a cleaner toolset: always-valid p-values and confidence sequences remain valid under continuous monitoring. That is the right framework for a live trading signal that you will naturally observe and update in real time. citeturn13search9turn13search14turn13search0

The key implementation rule is: every alert must be evaluated from an **immutable fire-time state**. That means fire-time features only; fire-time NBBO only; fire-time OI only; and a frozen trade-side/open-close classification as of that timestamp. No retroactive reclassification once later quotes, later open-interest updates, later news tags, or later “dealer regime” estimates arrive. The exact survivorship/look-ahead traps differ by vendor, but the papers above make the meta-point clear: strategy research overstates skill whenever ex post information leaks into the sort or score. citeturn12search3turn12search16turn13search9

Applying that standard to your own result, the adversarial interpretation is straightforward. If your reported “SOE A” win rate is below your breakeven rate and even the 95% conservative upper bound stays below breakeven, then **the current version of the grade should be treated as unproven or negative-EV until rebutted by a frozen, untouched validation sample**. It is not enough to say “but the intuitive pattern still looks right.” In finance, that sentence is often just a polite form of p-hacking. The only clean rebuttal would be a better predeclared target metric, or a sharply narrower regime defined **before** looking at the rescue result. citeturn12search16turn12search13

On your De Silva question, the answer is: **directionally yes, universally no**. De Silva and coauthors show that retail investors are pulled into option buying ahead of high-volatility earnings announcements and pay hefty premiums and large spreads for that exposure. That is strong evidence that “following obvious, binary catalyst flow that is visible to everyone” can be structurally bad. But Weinbaum, Fodor, Muravyev, and Cremers also show that option purchases are informative on **news days** and ahead of **unscheduled** events, whereas sales predict returns ahead of **scheduled** releases. So catalyst-flow-following is not always negative-EV; it is most likely negative-EV when the catalyst is already scheduled, crowded, retailable, and expensive. citeturn46search4turn46search6turn23view0

## Dealer positioning without pseudo-precision

The strongest case **for** dealer-gamma regime as a real signal is that there is now serious empirical support for it. Barbon and Buraschi document that negative ex-ante gamma imbalance interacts with underlying illiquidity to generate intraday momentum, while positive gamma relates to reversal, and they link gamma imbalance to flash-crash frequency and severity. The recent 0DTE literature also supports the direction of the mechanism: market-maker inventory gamma is related to future intraday volatility and to whether intraday returns tend to mean-revert or continue. That is not astrology. There is real signal there. citeturn42view0turn43search0turn43search1

The strongest case **against** your dealer-positioning layer is that the precision is usually fake. Egebjerg and Kokholm argue that focusing on the gamma effect alone understates the role of the **inventory effect** from changes in option market makers’ net positions. O’Donovan and Yu likewise emphasize that dealer hedging transmits inventory imbalances into stock liquidity. If you estimate “dealer positioning” from a static convention plus a rough OI transformation, you may correctly identify broad regimes while being very wrong about the exact flip level, the true sign of incremental hedging demand, or the timing of rebalancing. So dealer gamma is often useful as a **coarse state variable**, but much less trustworthy as a finely tuned price target or decimal-precise intraday pin forecast. citeturn44search4turn38search18turn44search0

That is why I would separate **structural positioning** from **same-day flow shock**. The literature on gamma imbalance and expiration effects is fundamentally about inventories and open positions, which makes **settled OI** the cleaner base for a structural read. Same-day volume should be layered on top as a separate shock variable only if you have high-confidence side/open-close labels. If you fold noisy same-day volume into “volume-adjusted OI” and then call the result structural GEX, you are mixing slow inventory with noisy print classification and creating a fertile ground for overfitting. citeturn42view0turn44search4turn38search24turn38search2

Your question about convention inversion is important because a fixed “calls-long / puts-short” dealer convention is exactly the kind of shortcut that becomes dangerous in modern single names. Recent work on retail options and intermediary hedging emphasizes that customer option demand leaves liquidity providers warehousing imbalances and hedging them in the stock. In retail call-heavy names, that can easily make the static convention wrong. You should assume the sign must be inferred from **actual opening flow and inventory evolution**, not from contract type alone. If you do not know who was opening, who was closing, and who initiated the trade, your “dealer sign” may be right on average and wrong on the days that matter. citeturn11search18turn44search0turn44search8

On **GEX × VEX × CEX**, the evidence is asymmetric. There is classic, reasonably solid evidence for gamma-related expiration effects; Ni, Pearson, and Poteshman show stock-price clustering on option expiration dates, which is consistent with pinning-type mechanisms. By contrast, the direct empirical case that **vanna** and **charm** add independent intraday signal beyond gamma is still much thinner and leans heavily on recent working papers rather than mature peer-reviewed evidence. That does not mean VEX/CEX are useless. It means they should be treated as **secondary overlays** or explanatory diagnostics, not primary drivers of a live conviction score unless you can prove incremental out-of-sample value yourself. citeturn9search3turn45search3

So the adversarial answer to “is dealer gamma real or astrology?” is: **the sign/regime is real enough to use; the fine-grained precision is often astrology**. Use it to decide whether to fade or follow intraday moves, whether to expect pinning or slippage, and whether to shrink or widen the useful half-life of flow. Do **not** treat your computed flip line, charm anchor, or vanna estimate as if it were measured truth. citeturn42view0turn44search4turn9search3

## How much latency actually matters

The literature does not support a simple “speed wins” story by itself. Bergsma and Lin show that option trading in the first 30 minutes after the open predicts returns over the remainder of the trading day, which means some informational edge can persist beyond seconds. Other work also argues that options can meaningfully contribute to price discovery, especially around information events. But Muravyev’s evidence on option-implied stock prices makes the opposite point for another class of signal: option **quotes** that disagree with stock prices do not predict future stock prices beyond what is already in the stock market. That is the cleanest academic way to say: **being fast on public option observations is not automatically alpha**. citeturn21search0turn39search14turn39search10

My best read of the literature is that the half-life of a flow signal depends much more on **what kind of flow it is** than on the raw timestamp lead over public scanners. For single-name, buyer-initiated, opening flow around unscheduled information, the alpha can plausibly persist from minutes into the rest of the day; for broad liquid index flow and plain-vanilla public prints, the market often internalizes the signal much faster, and what remains is mostly a regime/hedging effect rather than a pure informational one. That is why a 10–90 minute lead over social-media-style flow products may be meaningful in a few narrow buckets and close to meaningless in the median bucket. citeturn23view0turn21search0turn32view0turn42view0

So the adversarial answer to your speed claim is: **a latency edge is only valuable if the thing you are seeing early is genuinely informative**. If the trade is mostly retail leverage demand, a hedge roll, a borrow-fee proxy, or mislabeled side, then beating a tweet bot by 30 minutes changes very little. If the trade is a signed opening position in a coherent contract cluster ahead of unscheduled information, then sub-30-second detection probably does matter. In that sense, **selection quality dominates average latency**, while latency matters mainly inside your best-selected slices. citeturn30view0turn33view0turn47view0turn39search10

## Where an edge may still survive your own attack

A defensible surviving edge likely exists in **single-name, information-like flow**, but only after aggressive filtration. The cleanest candidate is: buyer-initiated **opening** trades, concentrated in a coherent cross-strike package, in names where information asymmetry or short-sale frictions matter, and especially around **unscheduled** news rather than scheduled retail magnets. Your scoring should prioritize side confidence, open/close confidence, strike coherence, and context, while heavily discounting anything that looks like scheduled-event retail lottery demand. citeturn23view0turn24view0turn30view0turn33view0turn47view0turn46search4

A second surviving edge likely exists in **contract geometry** rather than in “whale worship.” The papers favor strike-location and maturity choice, not the largest sticker notional. That means a multi-strike cluster aligned with a plausible informed trade thesis is more defensible than a single giant sweep. In practice, that implies your detection system should probably demote isolated large prints and promote **structured clusters** whose strike distribution implies consistent directional delta demand. citeturn33view0turn40view0turn26search1turn32view0

A third edge is **regime execution**, not direction forecasting. Dealer gamma is most useful as a way to condition the response to flow: negative gamma and poor liquidity tell you to respect continuation risk; positive gamma tells you to expect more mean reversion and shorter flow half-lives. That is a real intraday tool, but it is not a standalone directional oracle. Your best use of it is probably as a veto or sizing layer on top of the flow signal, not as another ingredient in a grand unified “conviction score.” citeturn42view0turn43search0turn44search0

The failure modes you are most likely still underestimating are these. First, **trade-side and open/close misclassification** can flip the sign of the very feature you care about. Second, **whale bias** can blind you to stealth-informed fragmentation while overemphasizing flashy but non-informative blocks. Third, **short-sale constraints and borrow fees** can make an option signal look directional when it is really a financing-friction proxy. Fourth, **scheduled catalyst flow** can be crowded, retail-heavy, spread-rich, and negative-EV to follow. Fifth, **dealer-position pseudo-precision** can make a broadly useful regime proxy look more exact than it is. Sixth, **multiple-testing and live tweaking** can manufacture conviction grades that feel stable but are only rearrangements of noise. citeturn38search24turn40view0turn47view0turn46search6turn42view0turn12search16turn12search3

## Open questions and limitations

There are still important places where the published evidence is thinner than traders often assume. I did **not** find a clean, mature, peer-reviewed horse race that directly tests **settled OI versus your exact volume-adjusted-OI formula** for dealer GEX in the way you want. The strongest published work supports gamma/inventory/liquidity mechanisms, but not your precise transformation. citeturn42view0turn44search4

Likewise, I did **not** find comparably mature peer-reviewed evidence that **listed-option sweep tags, ISO-style urgency labels, or multi-tenor laddering** are standalone predictors of single-name forward returns. The closest evidence is stronger for equities ISOs and for contract-type selection under information/leverage considerations than for those exact dashboard features. Treat those fields as heuristics until you can validate them on untouched data. citeturn37search7turn30view0turn33view0

Finally, the evidence that **VEX and CEX** add robust, independent signal beyond GEX is still notably weaker than the evidence for gamma itself and for expiration-related pinning. If you keep them, they should earn their place by demonstrable incremental out-of-sample value, not by theoretical elegance. citeturn9search3turn45search3turn42view0

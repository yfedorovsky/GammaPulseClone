## Academic foundation

**TL;DR:** The academic canon is clear that options markets often impound information before the underlying stock fully reflects it. What is much less established is the specific claim that **same-day coordinated multi-name options positioning** is systematically stronger than single-name flow; the closest literature supports cross-firm information diffusion and rival-firm pre-positioning, but not yet a canonical “public-tape cluster” model. citeturn14view0turn16view0turn17view0turn11search1turn19view2

The single-name canon starts with **Pan & Poteshman (2006, RFS)**: using a unique options dataset, they show that a low put-call ratio based on **buyer-initiated opening trades** predicts positive stock returns of **more than 40 basis points the next day** and **more than 1% over the next week**, with stronger predictability where informed-trader concentration is higher and option leverage is greater. In other words, the canonical finding is not merely that “options are active before news,” but that *signed opening flow* in options contains economically large, short-horizon information that is not yet in the stock. citeturn14view0

A second pillar is **Cremers & Weinbaum (2010, JFQA)**. They show that deviations from put-call parity, measured through the call-put implied-volatility spread, predict future returns: between **January 1996 and December 2005**, a portfolio long stocks with relatively expensive calls and short stocks with relatively expensive puts earns about **50 basis points per week** on a value-weighted, five-factor-adjusted basis, with a **t-statistic of 3.76**; importantly, the long side earns about **23 bps** and the short side about **-26 bps**, which is why they argue the result is not just a hard-to-borrow or short-sale-constraint story. That paper matters for your thesis because it says **option pricing distortions themselves**, not just raw volume, can reveal informed direction. citeturn16view0turn16view1

A third pillar is **Roll, Schwartz & Subrahmanyam (2010, JFE)**, who formalize the **options-to-stock volume ratio (O/S)**. Using data from **1996 to 2007**, they show O/S is higher around earnings announcements and that higher pre-announcement O/S predicts larger post-announcement abnormal moves; they also report evidence that some options traders are trading in the right direction ahead of the surprise, which they interpret as options activity improving market efficiency. This is a softer directional signal than Pan & Poteshman, but it is important because it moves the literature from “signed trade classification” to a more publicly observable relative-activity measure. citeturn14view1turn14view3

A fourth pillar is **Johnson & So (2012, JFE)**, which strengthens the O/S approach by showing that when trade direction is unobserved, relative options activity still predicts returns. In **1996–2010** weekly data, firms in the lowest decile of O/S outperform the highest decile by about **0.34% per week**—about **19.3% annualized**—with the effect stronger when short-sale costs are high; they also show O/S predicts future firm-specific earnings news, which is exactly the kind of result you would want if you are building a scanner meant to infer *informed* rather than merely *busy* flow. citeturn17view0

A fifth, later contribution is **Hu (2014, JFE, via SSRN abstract page reviewed)**, which finds that both option trades and option quotes predict future stock moves while stock orders, after controls, do not. Hu’s key contribution is the non-reversal result at longer horizons: the price impact does not simply mean-revert as you would expect from transient dealer hedging pressure, which supports the “private-information revelation” interpretation rather than a pure microstructure-noise story. citeturn15view0

The cross-firm literature gives the second half of your thesis. **Hou (2007, RFS)** shows that stock return lead-lag is predominantly an **intra-industry** phenomenon: big firms lead small firms within industries, the effect is stronger for negative information, and it is more pronounced in smaller, more neglected industries. That is directly relevant because your proposed signal is not just “two tickers traded together,” but “two economically linked, sector-clustered tickers traded together,” and Hou’s evidence says industry information diffusion is real and economically structured. citeturn11search1turn11search10

**Cohen & Frazzini (2008, JF)** extends this from industry to economic networks. Using disclosed customer-supplier links, they find a long-short strategy based on customer returns earns **about 1.55% per month**, or **18.6% annualized**, and that the underreaction closes only gradually; in their draft, they estimate suppliers initially absorb only about **60%** of the information shock, with the remaining **40%** reflected over subsequent months. That is highly relevant to your thesis because it says linked-firm return information is not redundant to ordinary industry momentum and that *related-name* signals can dominate naive sector clustering. citeturn13view0

Where the literature gets closest to your exact claim is in work on rivals and peer firms, but here the evidence is thinner and less canonical. **Tookes (2008)** develops the theory that informed traders can trade in competitors’ stocks around firm events, and later work such as **Mudalige et al. (2020)** finds evidence of informed trading in competing firms around earnings announcements; the most directly relevant options-market paper I found is **Clancey-Shang (2022)**, which documents that rivals of M&A firms experience abnormal stock and option volume before announcements and that a rival’s pre-announcement IV spread predicts its announcement-window CAR. That is meaningful support for your thesis, but it is also the limitation: the literature supports **spillover-informed trading across names**, not yet a universally accepted claim that a same-day, same-expiry, same-direction options cluster across tickers is *systematically* a stronger signal than the single-name signal from which it is built. citeturn21search1turn21search4turn19view2

## Market microstructure realism

**TL;DR:** Your proposed signal is **detectable in principle**, but not in the strong sense that a retail vendor can usually “see one coordinated order” on the public tape. In listed options, public data mostly reveal **correlated legs**, while a true cross-underlying parent order generally requires proprietary OMS/EMS metadata, broker data, or regulatory audit-trail linkage to identify with confidence. citeturn7search2turn40view1turn22search13turn17view0

The single most important market-structure fact is that exchange-recognized complex orders in listed equity options are generally defined within **the same underlying security**, not across two different underlyings. Nasdaq ISE’s rules define a complex order as the concurrent execution of two or more option series in the **same underlying security**, and Cboe’s complex-order book language similarly centers the same-underlying case; so a MU-plus-TSM package is not something the public options market typically stamps with a native “this was one multi-name parent order” label. For your thesis, that means the strongest product claim is not “we detect one official cross-ticker complex order,” but “we infer a high-probability coordinated cluster from separate but synchronized legs.” citeturn7search2turn7search3

Regulators can get closer because the CAT regime carries more linkage than the retail tape. The CAT FDID primer says FDIDs are required on all new-order events and that the Plan Processor associates an FDID with the order lifecycle, including trades, via lifecycle linkage; but it also notes that allocations are not required to be linked to particular orders or executions and that institutional orders may use a relationship ID when the final account structure is not known at receipt. In practice, that means CAT makes cross-ticker reconstruction **more feasible for surveillance**, but even CAT is not the same thing as a clean public parent-order ID that a retail unusual-flow vendor can trivially surface as one object. citeturn40view1

The TCA literature and practitioner writing point the same way. BestEx’s “order stitching” note explains that analysts often need to stitch multiple waves together to evaluate a **single parent order** accurately, and Johnson & So explicitly note that outside observers usually cannot see the underlying private-information mapping cleanly, especially when trading is split across multiple venues. The implication is straightforward: same-day same-direction sweeps across two names may indeed come from one institution and one thesis, but unless the vendor controls rich execution metadata, the vendor is inferring the relationship from timing, side, tenor, notional, venue pattern, and sector context. citeturn22search13turn17view0

On documented precedents, both regulators and academics have recognized cross-name positioning ahead of catalysts. The cleanest regulatory example is **SEC v. Panuwat**: the SEC alleged, and a jury later found, that Matthew Panuwat used confidential news about his employer **Medivation** to buy short-dated out-of-the-money call options on **urlIncytehttps://www.incyte.com**, a peer oncology company; the SEC complaint says he bought the calls within minutes of learning of the pending **urlPfizerhttps://www.pfizer.com** acquisition, that Incyte’s stock rose about **8%** on the deal news, and that the options roughly doubled, producing about **$107,066** in profit. Academic work extends the same logic to rival firms more broadly: Clancey-Shang (2022) documents abnormal option activity in non-announcing rivals before M&A announcements and predictive IV spreads on those rivals. citeturn24view0turn25search0turn25search11turn19view2

The false-positive problem is real and is the best empirical rebuttal to an overconfident version of the thesis. ETF research shows that common basket membership creates comovement beyond fundamentals: **Da & Shive** find common ETF membership drives return comovement, **Israeli, Lee & Sridharan (2017)** find that a one-percentage-point increase in ETF ownership is associated with roughly a **4% increase in return synchronicity** and a **14% decline in future earnings-response coefficients**, and **Ben-David, Franzoni & Moussawi (2018)** estimate that a one-standard-deviation increase in ETF ownership raises daily volatility by about **16%**. Add in evidence of positive cross-asset order-imbalance correlation even outside crises, and the practical takeaway is that many flows will *look* coordinated across semiconductor or biotech names simply because sector baskets, hedges, dispersion trades, and ETF arbitrage mechanically induce correlation. citeturn35view2turn35view3turn10search10turn9search15

So the microstructure verdict is nuanced. A detector that says “these two sweeps are very likely part of the same conviction expression” is plausible and, with careful conditioning, probably useful; a detector that says “these two sweeps are one provable institutional order” is usually too strong for public-feed data. citeturn7search2turn40view1turn22search13

## Comparable historical precedents

**TL;DR:** The best historical precedents show that cross-name options positioning can matter, but only one of the major cases I found is an unambiguous regulatory case and only one is a clearly attributable institutional macro trade. The rest are informative precisely because they show how hard attribution is: cross-name options bursts can be smart, illegal, social, hedging-driven, or simply reactive. citeturn24view0turn25search0turn26search5turn33search0

The clearest “savvy, not prosecuted” case is the **2020 mega-cap tech call book** associated with **urlSoftBank Grouphttps://group.softbank/en/**. Reuters, citing the contemporaneous reporting, said SoftBank bought roughly **$4 billion** of call options on U.S. technology names, which created about **$50 billion of exposure**, and later reporting tied the trade to the “Nasdaq whale” narrative as dealers hedged the calls by buying underlying shares. The case is relevant because it was explicitly **multi-name**, concentrated in a technology cluster, and large enough to plausibly affect the broader sector tape; it was not prosecuted as insider trading, and the historical debate was about whether it was a brilliant convexity trade or a destabilizing gamma machine. citeturn26search5turn26search1turn26search20turn28search22

The clearest “prosecutable” case is **Panuwat’s 2016 shadow trade**, even though it was one peer rather than a full basket. The SEC alleged that after learning that **Medivation** would be acquired by **urlPfizerhttps://www.pfizer.com**, Panuwat bought short-term, out-of-the-money calls in **urlIncytehttps://www.incyte.com** because he expected the acquisition news to lift peer oncology valuations; the complaint says the options roughly doubled and produced about **$107,066** of illicit profits, and the SEC’s April 2024 litigation release says a jury found him liable. This is not evidence of a vendor-detectable basket, but it is very strong evidence that regulators themselves view **cross-name options positioning on economically linked peers** as a real and legally salient phenomenon. citeturn24view0turn25search0turn25search11

The broadest “market-structure, not insider” precedent is the entity["historical_event","GameStop short squeeze","U.S. meme-stock episode of January 2021"]. The SEC’s staff report says the episode involved not just **urlGameStophttps://www.gamestop.com** but multiple other meme names, and later academic work on the meme frenzy documents gamma-squeeze dynamics across a broader set including **AMC**, **BBBY**, **EXPR**, and **KOSS**. For your thesis this case cuts both ways: it proves that cross-name options-led moves can cluster and propagate, but it also shows that clustered options activity does **not** necessarily mean informed institutional conviction; it can also mean reflexive retail, social coordination, and dealer-hedging mechanics. citeturn33search0turn33search11turn33search14

A more ambiguous but still useful precedent is the **2023 regional-bank options complex**. Reuters reported intense trading in the **KRE** regional-bank ETF and in individual regional-bank names such as **First Republic** and **Western Alliance** during the banking panic, while Bloomberg reported record bets in KRE options in March. This is important as a cautionary analogue: yes, the tape showed synchronized sector-wide options expressions, but the public record makes them look more **reactive and two-sided** than uniquely prescient, which is exactly why a cross-ticker detector needs to separate “headlines and contagion” from “high-conviction informed anticipation.” citeturn27search0turn27search7turn27search12

The practical lesson across these precedents is that cross-name options patterns are real, but **ground-truth attribution is rare**. When you can attribute them—SoftBank, Panuwat—the lesson is powerful; when you cannot, the same pattern can be interpreted as savvy, reflexive, hedging-driven, or coincidental. citeturn26search5turn24view0turn33search11

## Counterargument and thesis verdict

**TL;DR:** Sector-ETF flow is **not** a full substitute for correlated single-name flow, but it is the most important baseline the thesis must beat. ETF flow captures common-factor, macro, and hedging information efficiently; correlated single-name flow is potentially richer because it can reveal **selection**, **relative conviction**, and **specificity of thesis**, but only if you can remove the ETF-induced comovement noise. citeturn35view1turn35view0turn35view3turn35view2

The substitute argument has real force. Research on industry ETFs argues that ETFs facilitate informed trading by allowing investors to hedge industry risk—Huang, O’Hara, and Zhong describe the “long-the-stock/short-the-ETF” logic—and other microstructure work shows that ETF or index instruments can improve **contemporaneous** price discovery for common information. If a trader’s view is mainly “AI semis go up” or “regional banks go down,” then ETF options in instruments like SMH, SOXX, or KRE may capture much of the information more cheaply and more cleanly than trying to infer a basket from separate single-name legs. citeturn35view1turn35view3

But the papers that compare stock and index-option behavior also show why ETF flow is not enough. Lemmon and colleagues find that stock-option demand and index-option demand have materially different trader composition and informational patterns; at the same time, ETF-ownership papers show that ETFs increase comovement, synchronicity, and non-fundamental volatility in constituents. That means sector-ETF flow is excellent at telling you **a sector factor is in motion**, but much worse at telling you **which exact names informed traders chose, which names they deliberately excluded, how they distributed premium across peers, and whether the conviction was expressed through selection rather than pure beta**. In your formulation, that selection layer is the entire point of cross-ticker conviction detection. citeturn35view0turn35view2turn35view3turn10search10

So the best answer is not “ETF flow or correlated single-name flow.” The right framing is hierarchical: **ETF flow is the baseline common-factor signal; cross-ticker single-name clustering is the potential incremental alpha layer**. A serious product should therefore treat ETF/sector flow as a required control variable, not as a rival hypothesis to ignore. citeturn35view1turn35view3turn35view2

Open questions remain. I did not find a published horse-race showing that “same-day, same-direction, same-expiry, sector-clustered single-name options flow” systematically outperforms ETF-flow baselines after controlling for factor exposures, dealer hedging, and news timing; nor did I find public evidence that a major vendor has already solved the ground-truth labeling problem using something like CAT-linked training data. That absence does **not** kill the thesis, but it does mean the thesis should be stated as a **research-backed product opportunity**, not as an already proven law of market structure.

My final verdict is that **cross-ticker conviction detection is a real product gap, but only in probabilistic form**. The literature strongly supports the ingredients—single-name informed options flow, cross-firm information diffusion, rival-firm pre-positioning—and current retail-facing vendors do not seem to systematically surface the combined object; however, the strongest version of the thesis is overstated because public options data usually cannot prove that two underlyings were one parent order, and sector/ETF/commonality effects create a substantial false-positive base. The most defensible version of the claim is therefore: **there is a genuine, under-served product opportunity in ranking high-probability coordinated flow clusters across related names, conditioned on same direction, same tenor, unusual size, and controls for ETF/sector flow—but it remains a difficult research problem rather than a trivial extension of today’s single-name scanners.** citeturn14view0turn16view0turn17view0turn19view2turn7search2turn40view1turn35view3turn35view2
